GDPR (AVG - Algemene Verordening Gegevensbescherming) Requirements for Small Businesses in Netherlands 2025

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), known as AVG in Dutch, applies to all businesses processing personal data, regardless of size.

Core Requirements for Small Businesses:

1. Legal Basis for Processing:
- Consent: Must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous
- Contract: Processing necessary for contract performance
- Legal obligation: Required by Dutch or EU law
- Legitimate interests: Must balance business interests with data subject rights

2. Data Subject Rights (Individuals can request):
- Right of access: Request copies of their personal data
- Right to rectification: Correct inaccurate data
- Right to erasure: "Right to be forgotten" under certain conditions
- Right to restrict processing: Limit how data is used
- Right to data portability: Receive data in machine-readable format
- Right to object: Object to processing based on legitimate interests

3. Documentation Requirements:
- Records of processing activities (mandatory for companies with 250+ employees, recommended for smaller)
- Privacy policy/statement for website and customer communications
- Data processing agreements with third-party processors
- Data breach notification procedures

4. Security Measures:
- Technical measures: Encryption, access controls, secure storage
- Organizational measures: Staff training, access policies, regular audits
- Appropriate to the risk level of the data being processed

5. Data Breach Notification:
- Report to Dutch Data Protection Authority (Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens - AP) within 72 hours
- Notify affected individuals if high risk to their rights and freedoms
- Document all breaches, even if notification not required

6. Data Protection Officer (DPO):
- Required if: core activities involve large-scale processing of special categories of data, or systematic monitoring
- Most small businesses do NOT need a DPO, but should designate a privacy contact person

7. International Transfers:
- Personal data can only be transferred outside EU/EEA with appropriate safeguards
- Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) are commonly used
- Adequacy decisions exist for certain countries (UK, Japan, etc.)

Practical Steps for Small Businesses:

1. Conduct a data audit:
- What personal data do you collect?
- Why do you collect it?
- Where is it stored?
- Who has access?
- How long do you keep it?

2. Update privacy notices:
- Clear, plain language
- Explain what data you collect and why
- Inform about rights
- Contact information for privacy inquiries

3. Implement basic security:
- Strong passwords and two-factor authentication
- Regular software updates
- Secure backups
- Access controls

4. Train staff:
- Basic GDPR awareness
- How to handle data subject requests
- What constitutes a data breach
- When to escalate issues

5. Review third-party contracts:
- Ensure processors (cloud services, payment processors, etc.) are GDPR compliant
- Have data processing agreements in place

Penalties for Non-Compliance:
- Up to 20 million euros or 4% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher
- Dutch AP focuses on education and guidance for small businesses, but can issue fines for serious violations
- Reputational damage can be significant

Resources for Small Businesses:
- Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens (AP) website: www.autoriteitpersoonsgegevens.nl
- Free GDPR compliance tools and templates available
- Consider consulting with a privacy lawyer for complex situations

Key Dates and Deadlines:
- GDPR has been in effect since May 25, 2018
- No new requirements for 2025, but enforcement continues
- Regular reviews of compliance recommended annually

Special Considerations for Dutch Small Businesses:
- Dutch language privacy notices may be required for Dutch customers
- Consider the ePrivacy Directive for cookies and electronic marketing
- Sector-specific regulations may apply (healthcare, financial services, etc.)
